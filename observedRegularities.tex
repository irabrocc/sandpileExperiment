\section{Internal Color Periodicity}

Along any directional line, the height pattern repeats periodically without variation. For example, a line in direction $(2,1)$ always follows the sequence (Blue, White, Blue) from left to right.

\section{Frequency of Occurrence}

Lines with near-horizontal or near-vertical slopes appear less often. Most observed lines are horizontal, vertical, or at $45^\circ$ (slope $\pm1$). Lines with slopes $1/2$, $1/3$, $2$, or $3$ are rare.

\section{Vector Sum Rule}

When several directional lines meet at a point, their direction vectors sum to zero. This vector sum rule governs the pattern geometry.

Starting from the two basic directions $(1,0)$ and $(0,1)$, the vector sum rule generates the remaining observed directions $(1,1)$, $(2,1)$, $(1,2)$, $(3,1)$, $(1,3)$. The examples below show how each new direction arises from an intersection of two earlier ones.

The intersection of $(1,0)$ and $(0,1)$ yields $(1,1)$.

\subsection{Explaining the Observed Patterns}

The zero-sum rule at intersections explains several earlier observations.

\paragraph{No irrational slopes.} Every directional line is born at an intersection of two existing lines, so its direction vector is an integer sum (or difference) of the two parent vectors. Starting from $(1,0)$ and $(0,1)$, repeated addition can only produce integer vectors $(n,m)$. Hence every slope $m/n$ is rational. An irrational slope would require an irrational vector component, which integer arithmetic can never produce. This explains why no line with an irrational slope appeared in any of the $10\,000$ trials.

\paragraph{Rarity of near-horizontal or near-vertical lines.} A line with slope $1/n$ (or $n$) for large $n$ requires building a direction vector with very different components. Under the zero-sum rule, this means adding the basic vertical direction repeatedly to a nearly horizontal line, which demands several successive intersections along the same path. Each extra intersection is rare, so directions such as $(1,3)$ or $(3,1)$ appear far less often than $(1,1)$, which results from a single intersection of the two most common lines, $(1,0)$ and $(0,1)$. By contrast, the horizontal, vertical, and $45^\circ$ directions arise from the simplest sums and appear almost everywhere.

\section{Squared-Norm Rule}

For each directional line, let $\mathcal{D}$ be the total height difference from the background over one full period, and let $v=(n,m)$ with $\gcd(n,m)=1$ be its direction vector. Across all trials and all observed directions,

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{D} = |v|^2 = n^2 + m^2 .
\end{equation}

We verify this for four representative directions.

For $v=(0,1)$, the repeating unit is a single cell of height $2$, so $\mathcal{D}=3-2=1=0^2+1^2$.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=7cm]{0_1}
\caption{Directional pattern $(0,1)$. Repeating unit: one cell of height $2$, $\mathcal{D}=1$.}
\end{figure}

For $v=(1,1)$, the repeating unit is a single cell of height $1$, so $\mathcal{D}=3-1=2=1^2+1^2$.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=7cm]{1_1}
\caption{Directional pattern $(1,1)$. Repeating unit: one cell of height $1$, $\mathcal{D}=2$.}
\end{figure}

For $v=(2,1)$, the repeating unit consists of three cells of heights $2,0,2$, so
\[
\mathcal{D} = (3-2)+(3-0)+(3-2) = 5 = 2^2+1^2.
\]

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=7cm]{2_1}
\caption{Directional pattern $(2,1)$. Repeating unit: heights $2,0,2$, $\mathcal{D}=5$.}
\end{figure}

For $v=(3,1)$, the repeating unit consists of seven cells of heights $2,2,0,3,0,2,2$, so
\[
\mathcal{D} = (3-2)+(3-2)+(3-0)+(3-3)+(3-0)+(3-2)+(3-2) = 10 = 3^2+1^2.
\]

\subsection{Trivial Case}

Each background cell can be viewed as a directional line with vector $(0,0)$, its repeating unit being the cell itself. The difference from the background is $\mathcal{D}=3-3=0=0^2+0^2$, which also satisfies the rule.
